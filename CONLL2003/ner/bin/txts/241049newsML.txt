PRESS DIGEST - British business press - Dec 6.

Prepared for Reuters by The Broadcast Monitoring Company

DAILY TELEGRAPH

-- REED SLUMPS FIVE PER CENT AFTER PLEDGE ON INVESTMENT

Shares in the Dutch-Anglo publishing giant, Reed Elsevier, have slumped five per cent, its largest one-day fall in years, as the market took fright at a company trading statement that some analysts saw as a profits warning. Chief executive Nigel Stapleton said that most of the news in the statement was upbeat and he referred to the company's plans to launch new electronic products.

-- DIRECTOR'S MOVE SPARKS CABLE AND WIRELESS CLASH WITH WPP

The world's largest marketing services group, WPP, and Cable and Wireless are involved in a row over the departure of Robert Lerwill, WPP's finance director to C&W. WPP rushed out a statement at dawn, saying that Mr Lerwill was taking up an important post outside the group. Cable and Wireless announced the move at midday and the company's spokesmen were clearly ruffled at having their news already released by WPP.

-- BSKYB'S DIGITAL LAUNCH IN DOUBT

The digital launch of BSkyB will be delayed until 1998 unless the company places its order for new decoder boxes within the next two weeks. The manufacturers of the boxes warn that lead times needed to make all the components will not stretch any further. BSkyB is angry with the new regulations for digital pay- television and there is a debate about whether the boxes should include a module to make them compatible with terrestrial channels.

THE TIMES

-- GEORGE EASES PRESSURE FOR EARLY RISE IN INTEREST RATES

Governor of the Bank of England, Eddie George, in a testimony to the Commons Treasury Select Committee, has said that he sees no urgent need for a dramatic rise in interest rates. He has suggested that he would like to see higher rates before the election. His comments were less severe on the need for tighter money that many pessimists in the City expected. Mr George also said that the Budget had not changed the views of the Bank that rates would have to rise if the Government is to hit its inflation target.

-- BIG PAYOUT CUT FEARED AFTER GAS DEMERGER

The demerger of British Gas into two separate trading and pipeline companies has been met with fears of a big cut in dividend by the City. Concerns emerged after the utility revealed details for the structure of the two new companies and announced it has agreed with British Petroleum the first renegotiation of take-or-pay gas contracts. Shares in British Gas surged with the news that it had secured a breakthrough in reducing its two to three billion stg liability on high-priced gas contracts.

-- GRANDMET PLANS SHARE BUYBACK

The food and drinks company, Grand Metropolitan, is to seek shareholder permission to launch a share buyback. Chief executive, John McGrath, said that a share buyback is definitely on the agenda, however, the company has to examine its tax options before deciding when to proceed. GrandMet hopes to buy back ten per cent of the company's share capital, worth 950 million stg. The company emphasises that it is unlikely to make such a large repurchase.

THE GUARDIAN

-- OFWAT APPLIES PLUNGER AS WATER COMPLAINTS GUSH

Ofwat, the water industry regulator, has published figures showing a doubling of interruptions to water supply, an increase in complaints from customers and wide regional variations in performance. This comes after independent research shows that average bills have risen by 42 per cent in the seven years since water companies were privatised. Director general of Ofwat, Ian Byatt, said not all companies had improved their services, leading to a slower overall improvement than in previous years.

-- FINANCIAL SERVICES NEEDS OVERHAUL

Colette Bowe, a senior financial regulator has admitted that a sweeping overhaul of regulation in the financial services industry is need to restore confidence among private investors. Ms Bowe, chief executive of the Personal Investment Authority, said that self-regulation no longer delivered the level of protection expected by investors. She advocated the creation of a designated agency that would be accountable to Parliament.

-- PACIFIC ISLAND TWO WALK FREE

Scotland Yard has freed two people arrested in connection with a 100 million dollar attempt to bankrupt the Pacific state of Vanuatu because the Crown would have had difficulties in mounting a prosecution in this country. Last week Britain returned the ten "promissory notes" to Vanuatu, thus removing the threat of bankruptcy. If they had been traded on the international markets they would have crippled the tiny state.

THE INDEPENDENT

-- NATIONAL POWER FACES 200 MILLION STG PENSION REPAYMENT

A landmark ruling by the Pensions Ombudsman means that National Power could be asked to repay more than 200 million stg it removed from its employees' pension scheme in one of a deluge of claims likely to hit the privatised electricity companies. The electricity companies are being asked by a spokesman for the campaign representing pension fund members for surplus cash out of the schemes for the employers' benefit to be replaced and the sum involved may exceed 500 million stg.

-- BRENT WALKER FINANCE MAN CONVICTED

Donald Anderson, a former finance director of Goldcrest, the former film-making subsidiary of Brent Walker, has been convicted of attempting to pervert the course of justice by organising a wide ranging cover up of the Brent Walker profits fraud. He has been convicted of creating false documents between August 1988 and October 1990 to cover up 19 million stg of bogus profits reported by the group between 1984 and 1987.

- HANSON SPENDS 150 MILLION STG ON SPLITTING BUSINESS INTO FOUR

Hanson have revealed that the cost for splitting the industrial conglomerate into four parts is to be 150 million stg. 78 million stg relates to fees and other charges relating to the transfer of bonds to the new companies and 72 million stg related to the demerger, lower than the original estimate of 95 million stg. Hanson have also announced record profits of 1.81 million stg for its last year before the break-up.
